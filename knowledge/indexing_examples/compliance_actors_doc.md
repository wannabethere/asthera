# Compliance Actors Documentation

## Compliance Manager

### Role Description

The Compliance Manager is a strategic role responsible for overseeing and coordinating an organization's compliance program across multiple regulatory frameworks and industry standards. This role requires a comprehensive understanding of various compliance requirements including SOC 2, HIPAA, ISO 27001, FEDRAMP, PCI DSS, GDPR, and other relevant frameworks. The Compliance Manager serves as the central point of coordination between business stakeholders, technical teams, auditors, and regulatory bodies, ensuring that compliance activities are aligned with business objectives and executed effectively. This role demands strong project management skills, regulatory knowledge, risk assessment capabilities, and the ability to translate complex compliance requirements into actionable business processes.

The Compliance Manager must maintain a holistic view of the organization's compliance posture, understanding how different frameworks overlap, where requirements conflict, and how compliance activities can be optimized to reduce redundancy while maintaining effectiveness. They are responsible for establishing compliance governance structures, defining compliance policies and procedures, coordinating compliance assessments and audits, managing relationships with external auditors and assessors, and reporting compliance status to executive leadership and board of directors. The role requires staying current with evolving regulatory requirements, industry best practices, and emerging compliance frameworks that might impact the organization.

### Actions They Are Supposed to Take

Compliance Managers are responsible for developing and maintaining comprehensive compliance programs that address all applicable regulatory and industry requirements. They must establish compliance governance frameworks that define roles, responsibilities, and accountability structures for compliance activities across the organization. This includes creating compliance policies and procedures that clearly articulate requirements, expectations, and processes for maintaining compliance. They coordinate regular compliance assessments to evaluate the effectiveness of controls, identify gaps, and assess overall compliance posture. Compliance Managers manage the entire audit lifecycle, from initial planning through audit execution, finding remediation, and follow-up activities.

They are responsible for maintaining relationships with external auditors, assessors, and regulatory bodies, serving as the primary point of contact for compliance-related inquiries and communications. Compliance Managers develop and execute compliance training programs to ensure that all relevant personnel understand compliance requirements and their responsibilities. They establish and maintain compliance documentation systems that organize evidence, policies, procedures, and assessment results in a manner that supports audit activities and demonstrates compliance. They coordinate remediation activities when compliance gaps are identified, ensuring that issues are addressed in a timely and effective manner.

Compliance Managers conduct regular compliance risk assessments to identify areas where the organization might be exposed to compliance risks, evaluating both the likelihood and potential impact of compliance failures. They develop compliance metrics and key performance indicators that help track compliance program effectiveness and identify trends that might indicate emerging issues. They prepare and present compliance reports to executive leadership, board of directors, and other stakeholders, providing clear and actionable information about compliance status, risks, and resource needs. They manage compliance budgets and resources, ensuring that compliance activities are adequately funded and that investments in compliance deliver appropriate value.

### What They Want

Compliance Managers want comprehensive visibility into the organization's compliance posture across all applicable frameworks and requirements. They need clear, accurate, and timely information about compliance status, control effectiveness, and emerging risks that enables them to make informed decisions and provide accurate reporting to stakeholders. They want efficient and effective processes for managing compliance activities that minimize administrative burden while ensuring thoroughness and accuracy. They need tools and systems that help them coordinate compliance activities across large, distributed organizations, tracking progress, managing deadlines, and ensuring accountability.

They want strong support from executive leadership and business stakeholders who understand the value of compliance and provide adequate resources and authority to execute compliance programs effectively. They need technical teams that understand compliance requirements and can implement controls effectively, and they want clear communication channels that enable effective collaboration between compliance, security, IT, and business teams. They want external auditors and assessors who are professional, thorough, and fair, and who provide constructive feedback that helps improve compliance programs.

Compliance Managers want compliance frameworks and requirements that are clear, consistent, and practical, enabling them to implement effective compliance programs without excessive ambiguity or conflicting requirements. They want industry best practices and guidance that help them understand how to implement compliance requirements effectively and efficiently. They want compliance tools and technologies that automate routine compliance activities, reduce manual effort, and provide better visibility and control over compliance processes.

### What They Look For When There Is Compliance

When evaluating compliance, Compliance Managers look for comprehensive evidence that demonstrates both the design and operating effectiveness of controls. They examine control documentation to ensure that controls are properly designed, clearly documented, and aligned with compliance requirements. They review evidence that shows controls are operating as designed, including logs, reports, test results, and other documentation that demonstrates control effectiveness. They assess whether controls are being consistently applied across the organization, ensuring that compliance is not just achieved in some areas but maintained organization-wide.

They look for clear accountability structures that ensure someone is responsible for each control and that responsibility is clearly understood and accepted. They examine whether controls are being tested regularly and whether test results demonstrate ongoing effectiveness. They assess whether remediation activities are being executed effectively when issues are identified, ensuring that problems are addressed promptly and completely. They evaluate whether compliance training is effective and whether personnel understand their compliance responsibilities.

Compliance Managers look for metrics and indicators that demonstrate compliance program effectiveness, including trends that show whether compliance is improving or deteriorating over time. They examine whether compliance activities are integrated into normal business processes rather than being treated as separate activities that happen only when audits are approaching. They assess whether compliance investments are producing appropriate returns, ensuring that resources are being used effectively.

They look for strong relationships with auditors and assessors, positive audit outcomes, and constructive feedback that helps improve compliance programs. They examine whether compliance activities are sustainable over the long term, ensuring that compliance programs can be maintained without excessive burden or cost. They assess whether compliance programs are adaptable and can respond effectively to changing requirements, business conditions, and risk environments.

## Compliance Security Engineer

### Role Description

The Compliance Security Engineer is a technical role that bridges the gap between security engineering and compliance requirements, responsible for implementing and maintaining security controls that meet compliance standards. This role requires deep technical expertise in information security, system architecture, and security technologies, combined with a thorough understanding of compliance frameworks and how technical controls map to compliance requirements. The Compliance Security Engineer works closely with security engineers, system administrators, and compliance managers to ensure that security controls are designed, implemented, and operated in ways that satisfy both security objectives and compliance requirements.

This role demands expertise in multiple security domains including access control, encryption, network security, system monitoring, incident response, vulnerability management, and security architecture. Compliance Security Engineers must understand how security controls map to specific compliance requirements, how to implement controls that satisfy multiple compliance frameworks simultaneously, and how to demonstrate control effectiveness through technical evidence. They are responsible for translating compliance requirements into technical specifications, implementing controls according to those specifications, testing control effectiveness, and maintaining controls over time.

### Actions They Are Supposed to Take

Compliance Security Engineers are responsible for implementing security controls that meet compliance requirements, working with security and IT teams to design, deploy, and configure security technologies and processes. They conduct technical assessments to evaluate whether existing security controls meet compliance requirements, identifying gaps and developing remediation plans. They implement new security controls when compliance requirements demand them, ensuring that controls are properly designed, tested, and documented. They configure security systems and tools to generate the logs, reports, and evidence needed to demonstrate compliance.

They develop and maintain technical documentation that describes how security controls are implemented, how they operate, and how they meet compliance requirements. They conduct technical testing of security controls to verify that they operate as designed and effectively address compliance requirements. They work with system administrators and security engineers to ensure that security controls are properly maintained, updated, and monitored. They respond to technical questions from auditors and assessors, providing detailed explanations of how controls work and how they satisfy compliance requirements.

Compliance Security Engineers coordinate with security operations teams to ensure that security monitoring, incident response, and other security processes generate appropriate evidence for compliance purposes. They implement security automation and tooling that helps maintain compliance while reducing manual effort. They participate in security architecture reviews to ensure that new systems and applications are designed with compliance requirements in mind. They conduct security risk assessments that help identify technical risks that might impact compliance.

They work with compliance managers to understand compliance requirements and translate those requirements into technical implementation plans. They provide technical input to compliance policies and procedures, ensuring that requirements are technically feasible and clearly specified. They participate in compliance audits and assessments, providing technical expertise and evidence as needed. They track and remediate technical security issues that might impact compliance, ensuring that vulnerabilities and misconfigurations are addressed promptly.

### What They Want

Compliance Security Engineers want clear and specific compliance requirements that can be translated into technical implementations without excessive ambiguity. They need access to technical resources, tools, and expertise that enable them to implement security controls effectively. They want security technologies and platforms that support compliance requirements out of the box, reducing the need for custom implementations and complex workarounds. They need adequate time and resources to implement controls properly rather than being forced to take shortcuts that might compromise security or compliance.

They want strong collaboration with security engineers, system administrators, and other technical teams who understand compliance requirements and can help implement controls effectively. They need support from compliance managers who understand technical constraints and can help balance compliance requirements with technical feasibility. They want clear documentation and guidance about how to implement controls to meet compliance requirements, including examples, best practices, and reference architectures.

They want security tools and systems that generate appropriate logs, reports, and evidence automatically, reducing the need for manual evidence collection and documentation. They need testing tools and methodologies that help them verify control effectiveness efficiently. They want automation capabilities that help maintain compliance while reducing manual effort and human error. They need access to security intelligence and threat information that helps them understand security risks and implement appropriate controls.

Compliance Security Engineers want recognition that technical security work is essential for compliance and that their expertise is valued. They want opportunities to learn about new compliance requirements, security technologies, and best practices. They need clear career paths and professional development opportunities that recognize the unique combination of security and compliance expertise required for their role.

### What They Look For When There Is Compliance

When evaluating compliance from a technical perspective, Compliance Security Engineers look for security controls that are properly implemented, configured, and operating effectively. They examine technical documentation to ensure that controls are clearly described and that implementation details are accurate and complete. They review system configurations, security settings, and control implementations to verify that they match documented designs and meet compliance requirements. They assess whether controls are generating appropriate logs, reports, and evidence that demonstrate their effectiveness.

They look for security controls that are properly integrated into system architectures and operational processes, ensuring that controls work effectively in the actual operating environment. They examine whether controls are being maintained and updated appropriately, ensuring that security patches, configuration updates, and other maintenance activities are performed regularly. They assess whether security monitoring and testing activities are being conducted regularly and whether results demonstrate ongoing control effectiveness.

Compliance Security Engineers look for evidence that security controls are actually preventing or detecting security issues, not just that they exist and are configured correctly. They examine security incident logs, vulnerability scan results, and other security data to verify that controls are working as intended. They assess whether security processes are generating appropriate evidence for compliance purposes and whether that evidence is being collected, stored, and organized effectively.

They look for technical implementations that are sustainable over the long term, ensuring that controls can be maintained without excessive burden or cost. They examine whether security automation and tooling are being used effectively to maintain compliance while reducing manual effort. They assess whether technical teams understand compliance requirements and are implementing controls appropriately. They look for positive audit outcomes and constructive feedback from auditors that helps improve technical implementations.

## Security Engineer

### Role Description

The Security Engineer is a technical role responsible for designing, implementing, and maintaining security systems and controls that protect an organization's information assets and technology infrastructure. This role requires deep expertise in information security technologies, system architecture, network security, application security, and security operations. Security Engineers work across the entire technology stack, from network infrastructure and cloud platforms to applications and endpoints, implementing security controls that protect against threats while enabling business operations.

Security Engineers must understand both defensive and offensive security techniques, threat landscapes, attack vectors, and security best practices. They are responsible for implementing security technologies including firewalls, intrusion detection systems, security information and event management systems, identity and access management platforms, encryption systems, and vulnerability management tools. They work closely with system administrators, developers, network engineers, and other technical teams to ensure that security is integrated into all aspects of technology operations.

### Actions They Are Supposed to Take

Security Engineers are responsible for designing and implementing security architectures that protect organizational assets while enabling business operations. They conduct security assessments to identify vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, and security risks in systems, networks, and applications. They implement security controls including access controls, encryption, network segmentation, monitoring systems, and incident response capabilities. They configure and maintain security tools and platforms, ensuring that they operate effectively and generate appropriate security data.

They develop and maintain security documentation including security architectures, system designs, configuration standards, and operational procedures. They conduct security testing including vulnerability assessments, penetration testing, and security code reviews to identify and remediate security issues. They respond to security incidents, investigating threats, containing attacks, and remediating damage. They work with development teams to integrate security into the software development lifecycle, implementing secure coding practices and security testing.

Security Engineers monitor security systems and analyze security data to identify threats, vulnerabilities, and security incidents. They implement security automation and orchestration to improve security operations efficiency and effectiveness. They participate in security architecture reviews and design discussions to ensure that security is considered from the earliest stages of system design. They conduct security training and awareness activities to help technical teams understand security requirements and best practices.

They work with compliance and audit teams to ensure that security controls meet compliance requirements and generate appropriate evidence. They implement security controls that satisfy multiple compliance frameworks simultaneously, optimizing implementations to reduce redundancy while maintaining effectiveness. They participate in security risk assessments and help prioritize security investments based on risk and business impact. They stay current with security threats, vulnerabilities, and best practices, continuously improving security capabilities.

### What They Want

Security Engineers want clear security requirements and objectives that guide their work and help them prioritize security activities. They need access to security tools, technologies, and platforms that enable them to implement effective security controls. They want adequate resources including budget, time, and personnel to implement and maintain security controls properly. They need support from business stakeholders who understand the value of security and provide appropriate resources and authority.

They want strong collaboration with other technical teams who understand security requirements and can help implement security controls effectively. They need clear documentation and guidance about security requirements, best practices, and implementation approaches. They want security tools and platforms that are well-designed, reliable, and effective, reducing the need for workarounds and custom implementations. They need automation capabilities that help them manage security operations efficiently while reducing manual effort.

Security Engineers want recognition that security work is essential and that their expertise is valued. They want opportunities to learn about new security threats, technologies, and best practices. They need clear career paths and professional development opportunities. They want security incidents and vulnerabilities to be taken seriously and addressed promptly, ensuring that security risks are managed effectively.

They want compliance requirements that are clear, technically feasible, and aligned with security best practices. They need support from compliance teams who understand technical constraints and can help balance compliance requirements with security objectives. They want security controls that satisfy both security and compliance requirements without requiring separate implementations or excessive complexity.

### What They Look For When There Is Compliance

When evaluating compliance from a security engineering perspective, Security Engineers look for security controls that are properly implemented and operating effectively. They examine technical implementations to verify that controls are correctly configured and functioning as intended. They review security logs, monitoring data, and incident reports to assess whether controls are detecting and preventing security issues. They assess whether security processes are generating appropriate evidence for compliance purposes.

They look for security architectures and implementations that are well-designed, maintainable, and sustainable over the long term. They examine whether security controls are integrated effectively into system architectures and operational processes. They assess whether security tools and platforms are being used effectively and whether they're generating appropriate security data and evidence.

Security Engineers look for evidence that security controls are actually improving security posture, not just satisfying compliance checkboxes. They examine security metrics and trends to assess whether security is improving over time. They assess whether security incidents are being detected and responded to effectively. They look for positive security outcomes including reduced vulnerability counts, faster incident response times, and improved security posture.

They look for security implementations that are efficient and don't create excessive burden or complexity. They examine whether security automation is being used effectively to maintain controls while reducing manual effort. They assess whether security teams have the resources and capabilities needed to maintain security controls effectively. They look for recognition that security work is essential and that security engineers are valued contributors to compliance efforts.

## HR Team

### Role Description

The HR Team plays a critical role in compliance by managing human resources processes and policies that support compliance requirements related to personnel security, workforce management, and organizational governance. This role requires understanding of how HR processes intersect with compliance requirements including background checks, access provisioning, training requirements, policy acknowledgments, and workforce security. HR Teams work closely with security, compliance, and IT teams to ensure that personnel-related compliance requirements are met while maintaining effective workforce management practices.

HR Teams must understand compliance requirements related to personnel security, workforce training, access management, and organizational policies. They are responsible for implementing HR processes that support compliance including employee onboarding and offboarding procedures, background check processes, training administration, policy acknowledgment tracking, and access request workflows. They maintain HR records and documentation that serve as evidence for compliance purposes, ensuring that personnel-related compliance activities are properly documented and accessible for audits.

### Actions They Are Supposed to Take

HR Teams are responsible for implementing personnel security processes that support compliance requirements, including background check procedures, employee screening, and workforce clearance processes. They manage employee onboarding and offboarding procedures that ensure appropriate access provisioning and deprovisioning, supporting access control compliance requirements. They administer compliance training programs, tracking completion and ensuring that all relevant personnel receive required training. They manage policy acknowledgment processes, ensuring that employees acknowledge and understand organizational policies including security policies, privacy policies, and code of conduct.

They maintain HR records and documentation that serve as evidence for compliance, including training records, policy acknowledgments, background check results, and access request documentation. They coordinate with IT and security teams to ensure that access requests are processed appropriately and that access is granted and revoked in accordance with compliance requirements. They participate in compliance audits and assessments, providing HR documentation and evidence as needed. They develop and maintain HR policies and procedures that align with compliance requirements.

HR Teams conduct regular reviews of personnel-related compliance activities to ensure that processes are being followed and that documentation is complete. They identify and address gaps in personnel-related compliance processes, working with compliance and security teams to develop remediation plans. They provide training and guidance to HR staff about compliance requirements and how HR processes support compliance. They participate in compliance risk assessments, providing input about personnel-related risks and controls.

They work with compliance managers to understand compliance requirements and ensure that HR processes are designed to support those requirements. They coordinate with security teams to ensure that personnel security processes are aligned with security objectives. They maintain relationships with external vendors and service providers who support HR processes including background check providers and training vendors. They track and report on personnel-related compliance metrics including training completion rates, policy acknowledgment rates, and access request processing times.

### What They Want

HR Teams want clear compliance requirements related to personnel and workforce management that can be translated into HR processes and procedures. They need support from compliance and security teams who understand HR processes and can help design compliance-supporting procedures. They want HR systems and tools that support compliance requirements including training management systems, policy acknowledgment systems, and access request workflows. They need adequate resources to implement and maintain personnel-related compliance processes.

They want clear documentation and guidance about how HR processes should support compliance requirements, including examples and best practices. They need efficient processes that don't create excessive administrative burden while ensuring compliance. They want recognition that HR work is essential for compliance and that HR teams are valued partners in compliance efforts. They need opportunities to learn about compliance requirements and how they relate to HR processes.

HR Teams want HR systems that integrate with IT and security systems to support access management and other compliance-related workflows. They need automation capabilities that help manage compliance-related HR processes while reducing manual effort. They want clear accountability structures that ensure HR responsibilities for compliance are well-defined and understood. They need support from executive leadership who understand the importance of personnel-related compliance and provide appropriate resources.

### What They Look For When There Is Compliance

When evaluating compliance from an HR perspective, HR Teams look for personnel-related processes that are properly implemented and being followed consistently. They examine HR documentation to ensure that training records, policy acknowledgments, background check results, and other personnel-related evidence is complete and accurate. They review access request and provisioning processes to verify that they're operating effectively and generating appropriate documentation. They assess whether personnel-related compliance activities are being tracked and reported appropriately.

They look for HR processes that are efficient and don't create excessive burden for employees or HR staff while ensuring compliance. They examine whether HR systems and tools are supporting compliance requirements effectively. They assess whether personnel-related compliance training is effective and whether employees understand their compliance responsibilities. They look for positive audit outcomes related to personnel security and workforce management.

HR Teams look for evidence that personnel-related compliance processes are actually supporting security and compliance objectives, not just satisfying checkboxes. They examine metrics and trends related to training completion, policy acknowledgments, and access management to assess whether processes are effective. They assess whether personnel-related compliance activities are sustainable over the long term. They look for recognition that HR work is essential for compliance and that HR teams are valued partners in compliance efforts.

## Executive

### Role Description

The Executive role represents senior leadership including Chief Executive Officers, Chief Information Officers, Chief Security Officers, and other C-level executives who are ultimately responsible for organizational compliance and security. This role requires strategic thinking, risk management capabilities, and the ability to balance compliance requirements with business objectives. Executives must understand compliance at a strategic level, making decisions about compliance investments, resource allocation, and risk tolerance that impact the entire organization.

Executives are responsible for establishing the tone at the top regarding compliance, ensuring that compliance is treated as a business priority and that adequate resources are allocated to compliance activities. They must understand compliance risks and their potential business impact, making informed decisions about how to manage those risks. They are accountable to boards of directors, regulators, customers, and other stakeholders for organizational compliance, requiring them to understand and communicate compliance status effectively.

### Actions They Are Supposed to Take

Executives are responsible for establishing compliance governance structures that define how compliance is managed across the organization, including board oversight, executive sponsorship, and organizational accountability. They allocate resources to compliance activities, ensuring that compliance programs are adequately funded and staffed. They establish compliance policies and risk tolerance levels that guide compliance decision-making throughout the organization. They provide executive sponsorship for compliance initiatives, ensuring that compliance receives appropriate organizational attention and priority.

They review and approve compliance strategies, plans, and budgets, ensuring that compliance activities are aligned with business objectives. They receive and review compliance reports that provide information about compliance status, risks, and resource needs. They make decisions about compliance investments, balancing compliance requirements with other business priorities and resource constraints. They communicate compliance expectations to the organization, ensuring that all personnel understand that compliance is a business priority.

Executives participate in board-level compliance discussions, providing information about compliance status and risks to board members. They interact with regulators, auditors, and other external stakeholders regarding compliance matters. They make decisions about how to respond to compliance findings, audit recommendations, and regulatory requirements. They establish organizational culture and values that support compliance, ensuring that ethical behavior and compliance are core organizational values.

They review and approve compliance-related policies and procedures, ensuring that they're appropriate for the organization and aligned with business objectives. They make decisions about compliance risk tolerance, determining how much compliance risk the organization is willing to accept. They provide leadership during compliance incidents and crises, ensuring that the organization responds effectively. They recognize and reward compliance excellence, ensuring that compliance contributions are valued and recognized.

### What They Want

Executives want clear, concise, and actionable information about compliance status, risks, and resource needs that enables them to make informed decisions. They need compliance programs that are effective, efficient, and aligned with business objectives. They want compliance activities that don't unnecessarily burden business operations while ensuring that compliance requirements are met. They need assurance that compliance investments are producing appropriate returns and that compliance risks are being managed effectively.

They want strong compliance leadership and management that can execute compliance programs effectively without requiring excessive executive involvement in day-to-day activities. They need compliance teams that understand business objectives and can balance compliance requirements with business needs. They want positive audit outcomes and regulatory relationships that demonstrate effective compliance management. They need compliance programs that are sustainable over the long term and can adapt to changing requirements and business conditions.

Executives want compliance frameworks and requirements that are clear, consistent, and practical, enabling effective compliance without excessive ambiguity or complexity. They want industry best practices and guidance that help them understand how to implement compliance effectively. They need compliance tools and technologies that improve efficiency and effectiveness while reducing costs. They want recognition from stakeholders including customers, partners, and regulators that the organization takes compliance seriously and manages it effectively.

They want compliance programs that contribute to business value, not just satisfy regulatory requirements. They need compliance activities that are integrated into normal business processes rather than being treated as separate activities. They want compliance metrics and reporting that help them understand compliance effectiveness and make informed decisions. They need assurance that compliance risks are understood, managed, and within acceptable tolerance levels.

### What They Look For When There Is Compliance

When evaluating compliance, Executives look for evidence that compliance programs are effective and that compliance risks are being managed appropriately. They examine compliance reports and metrics to assess overall compliance posture and identify trends that might indicate emerging issues. They review audit outcomes and regulatory interactions to assess whether compliance is being managed effectively. They assess whether compliance investments are producing appropriate returns and whether resources are being used efficiently.

They look for compliance programs that are well-managed, with clear accountability, effective processes, and strong execution. They examine whether compliance activities are integrated into normal business operations rather than being treated as separate activities. They assess whether compliance culture is strong, with personnel understanding and supporting compliance requirements. They look for positive relationships with auditors, regulators, and other stakeholders that demonstrate effective compliance management.

Executives look for compliance programs that are sustainable and can adapt to changing requirements and business conditions. They examine whether compliance risks are understood and managed within acceptable tolerance levels. They assess whether compliance contributes to business value, helping the organization achieve its objectives while managing risks. They look for recognition from stakeholders that the organization manages compliance effectively.

They look for compliance leadership and management that can execute programs effectively and provide accurate, timely information about compliance status. They examine whether compliance teams have the resources and capabilities needed to manage compliance effectively. They assess whether compliance policies and procedures are appropriate and being followed consistently. They look for evidence that compliance is treated as a business priority and receives appropriate organizational attention and resources.
